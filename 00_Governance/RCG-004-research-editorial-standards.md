1. Purpose

This standard establishes the editorial principles governing all research assets produced within the Unified Composition Guide (UCG) Research Catalogue.

Where previous governance defines the purpose, workflow, and organization of research, this document defines the editorial qualities that transform research into durable institutional knowledge.

Its purpose is to ensure that research is:

- accurate
- understandable
- evidence-supported
- reusable
- editorially consistent
- suitable for long-term maintenance

---

2. Governing Principle

The objective of research is not to preserve information.

The objective of research is to preserve understanding.

Research should therefore produce knowledge that remains useful beyond the original source from which it was derived.

---

3. Editorial Philosophy

The Research Catalogue is not intended to function as a reading journal, notebook, or document archive.

Its purpose is to capture enduring understanding that reduces future research effort and strengthens the Unified Composition Guide.

Editors should continually transform information into progressively more reusable forms.

Research therefore moves through four editorial stages:

Information

↓

Understanding

↓

Knowledge

↓

Framework

Each stage represents increasing editorial refinement.

---

4. Editorial Objectives

Every research asset should strive to be:

- accurate
- concise
- technically precise
- evidence-supported
- reusable
- traceable
- framework-aware
- editorially neutral where appropriate

The editorial objective is clarity rather than completeness.

---

5. Editorial Maturity

Research assets mature through progressively higher editorial refinement.

Level 1 — Research Notes

Working observations captured during investigation.

Purpose:

Preserve thinking.

---

Level 2 — Structured Extraction

Individual reusable observations.

Purpose:

Capture reusable knowledge.

---

Level 3 — Knowledge Article

Editorial synthesis of multiple observations into coherent institutional knowledge.

Purpose:

Reduce future research.

---

Level 4 — Framework Contribution

Generalized principles suitable for incorporation into the Unified Composition Guide.

Purpose:

Improve the framework itself.

Editorial maturity should always move upward through these stages.

---

6. Writing Standards

Editors should:

- write declaratively
- distinguish observation from interpretation
- normalize terminology
- preserve technical accuracy
- avoid unnecessary repetition
- remove source-specific language where appropriate
- explain concepts rather than merely describe them
- prefer principles over isolated examples

Research should read as institutional knowledge rather than personal notes.

---

7. Evidence Standards

Significant editorial conclusions should indicate their evidentiary basis.

Common evidence sources include:

- Primary Sources
- Multiple Independent Sources
- Observed Practice
- Editorial Synthesis
- Experimental Investigation
- Working Hypothesis

Evidence should be proportional to the significance of the editorial claim.

---

8. Editorial Abstraction

Editors should continually evaluate whether knowledge can be generalized beyond the immediate source.

Editorial abstraction should preserve essential meaning while increasing future usefulness.

Examples include:

- transforming examples into principles
- transforming procedures into methodologies
- transforming observations into patterns
- transforming recurring patterns into framework opportunities

Abstraction should never sacrifice technical accuracy.

---

9. Editorial Compression

The purpose of editorial work is not to preserve the volume of information contained within a source.

Its purpose is to preserve the enduring value of that information in its most reusable form.

A lengthy source may ultimately produce:

- several Knowledge Articles
- multiple Extraction Records
- one Framework Candidate
- a small number of Subject Library updates

Successful editorial work reduces future effort while preserving future understanding.

---

10. Reusability Standard

Every completed Knowledge Article should satisfy a simple editorial question:

«Can another editor understand and reuse this knowledge without consulting the original source?»

If the answer is no, additional editorial refinement is required.

Reusability is the primary measure of editorial success.

---

11. Editorial Consistency

Editorial consistency strengthens institutional knowledge.

Editors should maintain consistency in:

- terminology
- formatting
- structure
- abstraction
- evidence presentation
- metadata
- cross-referencing

Consistency reduces editorial ambiguity across the Research Catalogue.

---

12. Common Editorial Errors

Editors should avoid:

- book reports
- document summaries that lack synthesis
- excessive quotation
- unsupported conclusions
- project-specific assumptions
- duplicated knowledge
- unnecessary historical narrative
- framework recommendations lacking editorial justification
- speculation presented as established knowledge

Research should preserve enduring understanding rather than reading experiences.

---

13. Editorial Completion

A research asset should generally be considered editorially complete when it is:

- accurate
- evidence-supported
- clearly written
- appropriately abstracted
- reusable
- traceable
- internally consistent
- reviewed
- ready for publication

Completion represents editorial readiness rather than intellectual finality.

Foundational Statement

«The enduring value of research is measured not by the quantity of information preserved, but by the quality of understanding retained. The purpose of editorial refinement is to transform information into knowledge, knowledge into institutional understanding, and institutional understanding into a foundation upon which future work can confidently build.»
