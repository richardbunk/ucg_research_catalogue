# RCG-002 — Research Extraction & Framework Codification Protocol

**Unified Composition Guide (UCG) Research Catalogue**

**Document ID:** RCG-002
**Title:** Research Extraction & Framework Codification Protocol
**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Canonical Foundational Governance
**Authority Level:** Core Operational Standard

---

# 1. Purpose

This protocol establishes the authoritative workflow by which research is evaluated, synthesized, codified, and incorporated into the Unified Composition Guide (UCG) ecosystem.

It governs the transformation of raw source material into durable institutional knowledge while preserving the distinction between accumulated research and reusable framework knowledge.

The protocol ensures that research is:

* repeatable
* reviewable
* evidence-based
* contextually appropriate
* reusable across future projects
* integrated into the UCG framework only where it provides lasting value

---

# 2. Governing Principle

Not every interesting fact deserves codification.

Only knowledge that meaningfully strengthens the UCG ecosystem should become part of permanent institutional knowledge.

Extraction is therefore an editorial process—not a transcription process.

---

# 3. Scope

This protocol applies to:

* books
* academic papers
* manuals
* technical standards
* historical works
* interviews
* documentaries
* websites
* conversations
* project postmortems
* experimental investigations
* long-form research

It applies equally to AI-assisted and human-led research.

---

# 4. Research Lifecycle

Every research effort progresses through seven operational stages.

```
Discovery
      ↓
Acquisition
      ↓
Evaluation
      ↓
Extraction
      ↓
Codification
      ↓
Editorial Review
      ↓
Publication
```

Each stage has distinct objectives, responsibilities, and exit criteria.

---

# 5. Stage One — Discovery

## Objective

Identify material that may contribute lasting knowledge.

Typical sources include:

* published works
* technical manuals
* archival collections
* standards organizations
* institutional documentation
* primary historical sources
* project experience
* expert interviews

### Exit Criteria

The source appears capable of contributing reusable knowledge.

---

# 6. Stage Two — Acquisition

## Objective

Capture and preserve the research source.

Possible forms include:

* PDF
* EPUB
* physical transcription
* OCR
* interview transcript
* web archive
* notes
* photographs
* audio transcription

The objective is preservation—not interpretation.

### Exit Criteria

Source material is accessible for repeated review.

---

# 7. Stage Three — Evaluation

## Objective

Determine whether the source merits systematic extraction.

Evaluation questions include:

* Is it authoritative?
* Is it unique?
* Is it historically significant?
* Does it solve recurring problems?
* Does it expand UCG capability?
* Will future projects benefit?

Possible outcomes include:

* Reject
* Archive Only
* Partial Extraction
* Complete Extraction

---

## Research Classification

Following evaluation, each source shall receive one classification.

* **Reference Only** — retained for consultation without planned extraction.
* **Background Research** — useful contextual material supporting future work.
* **Extractable** — contains reusable knowledge suitable for formal extraction.
* **Framework Candidate** — contains material likely to influence existing framework components.
* **Canonical Source** — foundational source expected to influence multiple areas of the UCG ecosystem.

These classifications help prioritize editorial effort and repository organization.

---

# 8. Stage Four — Extraction

Extraction identifies reusable knowledge at increasing levels of abstraction.

## Level A — Facts

Individual factual observations.

Examples:

* measurements
* dates
* terminology
* specifications

---

## Level B — Concepts

General principles.

Examples:

* engineering methods
* narrative structures
* artistic theory
* workflow models

---

## Level C — Systems

Connected groups of concepts forming operational processes.

Examples:

* editorial workflow
* architectural methodology
* quality assurance pipeline

---

## Level D — Framework Opportunities

Patterns sufficiently complete to justify potential UCG expansion.

Examples:

* new author personas
* visual authority systems
* research frameworks
* editorial standards
* contextual models

---

## Extraction Confidence

Each extracted observation should receive an editorial confidence assessment.

* **Verified** — directly supported by authoritative evidence.
* **Strongly Supported** — supported by multiple reliable sources.
* **Probable** — supported but requiring additional confirmation.
* **Tentative** — promising but incomplete.
* **Speculative** — retained for investigation but unsuitable for framework integration.

Confidence ratings assist future editors when revisiting research.

---

# 9. Codification Assessment

Each extracted item should be evaluated against the following questions.

Does it:

* eliminate future research?
* improve compositional quality?
* increase consistency?
* reduce ambiguity?
* generalize beyond the current project?
* integrate naturally into existing UCG systems?

If the answer is predominantly **No**, the material should remain research rather than framework.

---

## Framework Impact Assessment

Before codification, editors should determine the anticipated impact on the framework.

Impact classifications include:

* **No Impact** — affects only research records.
* **Minor Extension** — extends an existing module.
* **Cross-Module Impact** — affects multiple framework components.
* **Foundational Impact** — alters governance or architectural structure.

This assessment helps determine editorial workload, review requirements, and publication scope.

---

# 10. Framework Integration Targets

Knowledge approved for codification should be assigned to the most appropriate destination.

Examples include:

* Author Persona Library
* Context Matrix
* Behavior Registry
* Theme Archetypes
* Tone Registry
* Lexical Substitution
* Product Registry
* Subject Library
* Visual Authority Library
* Conversational Profile Library
* Knowledge Articles
* Governance Documents
* Future Module Proposals

Codification should always occur at the lowest level capable of supporting future reuse.

The complete set of valid integration targets is defined by the current UCG Framework Architecture. Additional destinations may be introduced through approved governance without requiring revision of this protocol.

---

# 11. Avoiding Framework Pollution

One of the greatest long-term risks is unnecessary expansion.

Editors should avoid introducing:

* duplicate concepts
* project-specific assumptions
* temporary trends
* overly narrow rules
* redundant registries
* speculative modules

Every addition increases maintenance burden.

Framework growth should therefore be deliberate, evidence-based, and editorially justified.

---

# 12. Editorial Codification

When research becomes framework knowledge, editors should:

* normalize terminology
* remove source-specific wording
* generalize where appropriate
* preserve technical precision
* record relationships to existing modules
* document rationale for inclusion

Codification transforms knowledge into infrastructure.

---

# 13. Traceability

Every codified element shall retain documented lineage.

```
Source
      ↓
Research Notes
      ↓
Extraction
      ↓
Editorial Review
      ↓
Codified Framework Component
```

Future editors should always be able to determine where framework knowledge originated.

---

# 14. Editorial Review

Before publication, reviewers shall confirm:

* factual accuracy
* editorial clarity
* appropriate abstraction
* absence of duplication
* correct destination module
* governance compliance

Only reviewed material becomes canonical.

---

# 15. Publication

Approved framework additions shall be published according to repository governance.

Publication includes:

* placement in the appropriate repository directory
* changelog updates
* version updates where required
* cross-reference updates
* index updates
* relationship updates

Publication completes the research lifecycle.

---

# 16. Framework Evolution and Deprecation

Framework knowledge evolves as better evidence becomes available.

When new evidence supersedes existing framework:

* previous versions remain archived
* rationale for supersession is documented
* cross-references are updated
* relationships are maintained
* historical traceability is preserved

Framework evolution shall occur through documented governance rather than silent revision.

---

# 17. Editorial Roles

Although one individual may perform multiple responsibilities, the research lifecycle distinguishes editorial functions.

### Researcher

Identifies, acquires, and documents research sources.

### Extractor

Identifies reusable knowledge and prepares structured extractions.

### Framework Editor

Determines whether extracted knowledge should become reusable framework.

### Architectural Editor

Verifies consistency with the broader UCG architecture and evaluates ecosystem impact.

### Publisher

Performs canonical publication, repository updates, indexing, and version management.

These roles establish responsibility without prescribing organizational structure.

---

# 18. Operational Philosophy

The Research Catalogue preserves knowledge.

The Framework codifies reusable knowledge.

These serve different purposes.

The Catalogue grows through accumulation.

The Framework grows through careful selection.

Maintaining this distinction prevents uncontrolled complexity while allowing institutional knowledge to expand indefinitely.

---

# 19. Relationship to RCG-001

RCG-001 defines:

> **What the Research Catalogue is.**

RCG-002 defines:

> **How research enters the Catalogue and how selected knowledge becomes part of the Unified Composition Guide.**

Together they establish the foundational governance governing research acquisition, evaluation, extraction, and framework integration throughout the UCG ecosystem.

---

# Foundational Statement

> Every source contains information. Few sources contain enduring knowledge. Fewer still contain principles worthy of becoming framework. The purpose of this protocol is to ensure that only carefully evaluated, evidence-supported, editorially refined knowledge becomes part of the Unified Composition Guide, preserving both the integrity of the framework and the long-term value of the Research Catalogue.
