---

## 1. Purpose

This protocol establishes the governance principles by which research quality is evaluated, validated, and maintained within the Unified Composition Guide (UCG) Research Catalogue.

Its purpose is to ensure that institutional knowledge remains reliable, transparent, evidence-supported, and suitable for long-term reuse.

Quality assurance is an ongoing editorial responsibility rather than a single review event.

---

## 2. Governing Principle

Institutional knowledge earns authority through demonstrated quality rather than publication alone.

Validation confirms that knowledge is sufficiently accurate, evidence-supported, architecturally consistent, and editorially complete to justify institutional reliance.

---

## 3. Scope

This protocol applies to all canonical research assets, including:

- Knowledge Articles
- Framework Candidates
- Governance Documents
- Canonical Framework Modules
- Subject Library entries
- Operational standards

Operational review procedures are governed separately by the RIP series.

---

## 4. Quality Objectives

Quality assurance seeks to preserve:

- factual accuracy
- editorial clarity
- evidence integrity
- architectural consistency
- traceability
- long-term maintainability

Quality exists to strengthen institutional confidence rather than create unnecessary process.

---

## 5. Validation Principles

Research validation should be:

- evidence-based
- repeatable
- proportional to the significance of the asset
- transparent
- documented
- editorially independent where practical

Validation is intended to improve knowledge rather than merely approve it.

---

## 6. Validation Criteria

Before an asset is considered suitable for canonical publication, reviewers should evaluate:

- factual correctness
- adequacy of supporting evidence
- editorial quality
- clarity of abstraction
- architectural consistency
- metadata completeness
- relationship integrity
- governance compliance

Validation confirms that an asset satisfies institutional expectations.

---

## 7. Editorial Confidence

Institutional knowledge may possess differing levels of editorial confidence.

Confidence reflects the current strength of supporting evidence rather than the value of the research itself.

Typical confidence classifications include:

- Verified
- Strongly Supported
- Probable
- Tentative
- Under Investigation

Confidence classifications should remain visible and subject to revision as new evidence emerges.

---

## 8. Continuous Validation

Validation is continuous rather than final.

New evidence may:

- strengthen existing knowledge
- clarify interpretation
- identify editorial improvements
- recommend supersession
- justify additional research

Institutional knowledge therefore remains capable of refinement throughout its lifecycle.

---

## 9. Validation Independence

Editorial review should evaluate research on its own merits.

Reviewers should distinguish between:

- evidence
- interpretation
- editorial synthesis
- framework recommendation

Validation strengthens editorial objectivity by separating observation from conclusion.

---

## 10. Correction and Improvement

Discovery of an error should initiate editorial improvement rather than diminish institutional confidence.

When issues are identified:

- corrections should be documented
- version history preserved
- supersession relationships updated where appropriate
- rationale recorded

Institutional trust is strengthened through transparent correction.

## Foundational Statement

> Institutional knowledge derives its authority not from permanence, but from continual editorial responsibility. The purpose of validation is not to declare knowledge infallible, but to ensure that it remains transparent, evidence-supported, and worthy of continued institutional confidence as understanding evolves.
